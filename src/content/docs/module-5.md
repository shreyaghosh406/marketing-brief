### Why this matters

This is where marketing stops being theory and meets real people scrolling on real apps. Every platform is its own little country — its own culture, its own unwritten rules, its own idea of what's worth rewarding. Outsiders who barge in with a sales pitch get ignored or worse. Locals who understand the place get heard. This module teaches you to become a local — first as a transferable skill, then on two specific, very different platforms: **X** and **LinkedIn**.

### The core ideas

**Platform thinking — the part that transfers everywhere.**

Before any specific platform, internalize these. They're true on X, LinkedIn, TikTok, Reddit, YouTube — all of them:

- **Every platform rewards native behavior and punishes spam.** "Native" means content that looks like it belongs, made by someone who clearly uses the platform. The fastest way to fail is to paste the same promotional blurb everywhere. People can smell it instantly, and so can the algorithm.
- **Value-first beats promo-first.** Give before you ask. Teach something, share something useful, be genuinely interesting — and earn the attention you later spend. The ratio matters: be useful far more often than you self-promote.
- **The algorithm rewards what humans reward.** Underneath the mystery, almost every feed is trying to show people content that *other people engaged with* — that earned replies, saves, shares, watch-time. Stop trying to trick the algorithm and start earning genuine engagement; the algorithm is mostly a mirror of that.
- **The path to mastery is the same everywhere: lurk → contribute → post → analyze.**
  1. **Lurk.** Spend real time reading before you post. Learn what the good stuff looks like and what falls flat.
  2. **Contribute.** Reply, comment, add value to others' posts. Become a familiar, helpful presence.
  3. **Post.** Now make your own, having absorbed the culture.
  4. **Analyze.** Watch what worked, do more of it. Repeat.

If you only remember one thing from this module: **respect the room before you speak in it.**

**Deep dive 1: X (formerly Twitter).**

X is a fast, public, conversation-driven platform. It's where ideas, news, and "building in public" live. Its culture rewards being interesting, sharp, and willing to engage.

What tends to work on X:

- **The first line is everything.** Feeds move fast and posts get truncated. If your opening line doesn't make someone stop, nothing else you wrote matters. Front-load the hook.
- **Conversation is the engine.** Replies — both getting them and giving them — are central. A post that sparks a discussion travels far further than a post that just broadcasts. Reply to others generously; it's how you get known.
- **Threads for depth.** When one post isn't enough, a thread (a chain of connected posts) lets you tell a longer story or teach something step by step. The first post still has to earn the click into the rest.
- **Build in public.** Sharing the real, specific journey of making something — the wins, the numbers, the stumbles — resonates strongly here. Specificity and honesty outperform polish.
- **Consistency and personality.** X rewards people who show up regularly with a recognizable voice, not brands that post sterile announcements.

What fails on X: corporate stiffness, obvious ads with no insight, posting into the void without ever engaging with anyone else.

**Deep dive 2: LinkedIn.**

LinkedIn is the near-opposite of X in culture, which is exactly why we pair them — learn both and you can read almost any platform. It's identity-first and professional: your real name, your career, your credibility are attached to everything.

What tends to work on LinkedIn:

- **Credibility and substance.** The audience is professionals. Insight, lessons learned, useful frameworks, and real results land well. Hot takes for their own sake land poorly.
- **A human voice inside a professional frame.** The best LinkedIn content is personal and story-driven *without* tipping into oversharing — a real lesson from real work, told like a person, not a press release.
- **Readable formatting.** Short paragraphs, clear structure, a strong opening line (the feed truncates here too). Walls of text get scrolled past.
- **Teaching and generosity.** Posts that help other professionals get better — and that invite thoughtful discussion in the comments — get rewarded with reach.
- **It's a long game.** LinkedIn is where you build professional reputation steadily over time. It compounds.

What fails on LinkedIn: empty engagement-bait, inauthentic "inspirational" fluff, and pure self-promotion with no value for the reader.

**Same idea, two platforms.** A lesson you learned shipping a project might become, on X, a punchy thread that opens with a surprising number — and on LinkedIn, a reflective story about what the experience taught you professionally. Same truth, re-tailored to the room. That re-tailoring *is* the skill.

### Worked example — good vs. bad

Imagine you want to share that a tool you use saved you hours on a tedious task.

**Bad (promo-first, non-native):**
> "🚀 Check out [Product]! It automates your workflow and saves you time! Sign up now at the link! #productivity #AI #automation"

Why it fails: it's an ad, it teaches nothing, it's the same on every platform, and everyone scrolls past on reflex.

**Good (value-first, native to X):**
> "Spent 3 years doing a 2-hour weekly task by hand. Last month I automated it in an afternoon. Here's the exact setup, and the one mistake that cost me a day 👇"

Why it works: a hook with a real number, a promise of genuine value, and an honest stumble that makes it credible. The product can appear *inside* the helpful story — earned, not announced.

### Common mistakes

- **Posting promo into a community before you've contributed anything.** You haven't earned the ask.
- **Copy-pasting one post across platforms.** What sings on X reads as alien on LinkedIn, and vice versa.
- **Ignoring the unwritten rules.** Every platform has norms that aren't in any FAQ. Lurking is how you learn them. Skip that step and you'll get it wrong.

### Your assignment — "Post for real"

Write **one genuinely postable contribution** — a post for either X or LinkedIn — on **any topic you can add real value to** (a thing you know, learned, or figured out). It must help the reader and read as native to the platform you chose. It should *not* read as an ad.

Then add **three sentences**: which platform you chose, who it's for, and why it fits there.

This one is portfolio-worthy — write something you'd actually be proud to publish.

**Rubric**

| Criterion | What we're looking for |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **Native fit** | It respects the chosen platform's culture, format, and voice. |
| **Value-first** | It helps the reader before it helps you. It earns attention. |
| **Survivability** | It would actually do fine if you posted it — not get ignored or flagged as spam. |

Rated **Developing / Solid / Strong.**

### Further reading (optional)

Pick one creator on X and one on LinkedIn whose content you admire. Study ten of their posts each. Notice the first lines. You're reverse-engineering native instinct.
