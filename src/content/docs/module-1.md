*This is the one module about the specific product you'll be working on. Everything after this is general marketing skill that works anywhere.*

### Why this matters

You cannot market something you don't understand. Not "sort of" understand — *understand*. The best marketers can explain their product to a stranger at a bus stop in thirty seconds and watch the stranger's face go "oh, I get it." That clarity is the foundation everything else is built on. If you're fuzzy on what the product does, your copy will be fuzzy, your targeting will be fuzzy, and your metrics won't mean anything. So we start here.

For this module, what you need to internalize isn't a slogan — it's how the thing actually works.

**Gravity is a two-sided marketplace.** That phrase sounds technical, but it's simple, and you already understand it from other products:

- Uber has two sides: **riders** who want a trip, and **drivers** who give the trip. Uber's job is to match them.
- YouTube has two sides: **viewers** who want to watch, and **creators** who make videos. YouTube's job is to match them.

Gravity works the same way. It has two sides:

- **Users** — people with a task they'd rather not do themselves. They describe the task in plain English ("find me ten leads in this industry and draft an intro email to each").
- **Builders** — people who build the AI agents that do those tasks. They publish their agents on Gravity and earn a share of the revenue every time their agent runs.

In between sits the **agent** — the thing that actually does the work. A user describes a task, Gravity matches it to the right agent, the agent runs, and the user gets a result.

Why does the two-sidedness matter so much to you as a marketer? Because you're not marketing to one audience — you're serving two. Users want their work done. Builders want to earn. A message that lands beautifully with one side might mean nothing to the other. Keep both in your head at all times.

**How a single job runs — request → match → run → result.**

1. **Request.** The user describes what they want, in plain language. No code, no menus.
2. **Match.** Gravity figures out which agent (built by some builder) is right for this job.
3. **Run.** The agent does the work — using tools, taking real actions, not just chatting.
4. **Result.** The user gets the finished output.

That loop is the whole product in miniature. When you market Gravity, you are, underneath everything, selling the feeling of that loop working.

### Worked example

Here's a job running end to end, so the loop feels concrete:

> A small business owner types: *"Pull the 20 most recent reviews of my café from Google, and tell me the three complaints that come up most."*
> Gravity **matches** this to an agent built to read and summarize reviews. The agent **runs** — it fetches the reviews, reads them, clusters the complaints. The **result** comes back: "Most common issues: slow service at lunch (8 mentions), oat milk often out of stock (5), bathroom cleanliness (4)."
> The owner spent fifteen seconds describing a task that would've taken them an hour. A builder earned a cut for building the agent that did it.

Notice both sides got value. That's the marketplace working.

### Common mistakes

- **Describing only the user side.** It's the more obvious half, so beginners forget builders exist. But the builders are why there's anything *to* match a task to. Always hold both sides.
- **Overpromising.** It's tempting to say the agent does *everything* perfectly. It doesn't, and honest marketing ages better than hype. (Module 2 will give you the vocabulary for being honest about what AI can and can't do.)
- **Reaching for internal jargon.** Words that make sense inside the company often mean nothing to an outsider. If your explanation needs a glossary, it's not an explanation yet.

### Your assignment — "Explain it to a friend"

Write a **100–150 word** explanation of Gravity for someone who has never heard of it — a friend, a parent, anyone outside tech. Then add **one sentence** describing who Gravity is *not* for.

Rules: no jargon you wouldn't use out loud. No slogans. Just make a stranger understand it.

**Rubric**

| Criterion | What we're looking for |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **Accuracy** | You get the product right — *including* the builder side, not just users. |
| **Clarity** | A total stranger would understand it. No unexplained jargon. |
| **Restraint** | You describe it honestly. You don't oversell or promise magic. |

Each is rated **Developing / Solid / Strong.** Aim for Strong on Clarity especially — it's the whole skill.
