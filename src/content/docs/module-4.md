### Why this matters

A great product that no one hears about, framed for the wrong people, on the wrong channel, dies quietly. "Go-to-market" is the plan that prevents that — the bridge between *the thing exists* and *the right people are using it*. Once you can think in GTM, you stop guessing ("let's post everywhere and hope") and start reasoning ("this audience, this channel, this message, this metric"). It's the strategic backbone under all the tactical work.

### The core ideas

**What GTM means, in one sentence.**

> Getting the **right product** to the **right people**, through the **right channel**, with the **right message**.

That's it. Everything below is just those four words unpacked.

**1. The right people (your ICP).**

ICP stands for **Ideal Customer Profile** — a clear picture of the specific person who most needs what you offer. Not "everyone." *Everyone* is not a target; it's a wish. The tighter your picture, the sharper every other decision gets.

A weak ICP: "people who want to be productive." A strong ICP: "freelance designers juggling 4+ clients who lose an hour a day to admin." See how the second one practically writes your message for you?

**2. Positioning.**

Positioning is the answer to "why this, instead of the alternative (including doing nothing)?" It's the single clearest reason your ICP should care. Good positioning is a choice — by saying you're *the* thing for X, you accept you're *not* the thing for Y. That trade is what makes it powerful.

**3. The right channel (and channel–product fit).**

A channel is *where* you reach people: search, a social platform, email, communities, word of mouth, paid ads. Not every channel fits every product. A serious B2B tool may thrive on LinkedIn and die on TikTok; a visual consumer app is the reverse. **Channel–product fit** means matching where you show up to where your ICP actually pays attention *and* where your product naturally makes sense. Chasing a channel just because it's hot is how budgets evaporate.

**4. The right message.**

The message is what you actually say — and it has to be tuned to both the audience and the channel. The same truth about your product gets phrased differently for a skeptical professional on LinkedIn than for someone scrolling fast on X. (Module 6 makes you good at the words themselves. GTM decides *what idea* the words should carry.)

**Sequencing — don't open every door at once.**

Beginners want to be everywhere immediately. Strong teams do the opposite: they pick **one** channel, learn to win on it, and only then add the next. Why?

- Focus beats spread. One channel done well outperforms five done badly.
- You learn faster. With one variable, you can actually tell what's working.
- You earn the right to scale. Once a channel reliably works, *then* you pour fuel on it and add the next.

Picture phases: start narrow and hands-on (founders talking directly to early users) → grow into a repeatable channel or two → only then layer on content engines, referrals, and paid. Each stage earns the next.

**Flywheels and moats.**

A **flywheel** is a loop where each turn makes the next turn easier — growth that compounds instead of needing to be re-bought every month. Classic example: more buyers attract more sellers, which brings more selection, which attracts more buyers. A **moat** is whatever makes that advantage hard for a competitor to copy. You don't need to engineer these as a beginner, but you should be able to spot them — because the channels and messages that feed a flywheel are worth more than ones that don't.

### Worked example

A small team launches a simple tool that auto-generates invoices for freelancers.

- **ICP:** solo freelancers who hate admin — not "small businesses" (too broad), not "enterprises" (wrong product).
- **Positioning:** "Invoicing that's done before you finish your coffee" — the one reason to care, aimed squarely at people who dread the task.
- **First channel:** communities where freelancers already gather to complain about exactly this pain — not a splashy paid campaign across five platforms. One channel, where the ICP lives.
- **Message there:** not "we have features" but "stop losing Sunday nights to invoicing."
- **Proof metric:** not signups — *activation* (did they actually send a first invoice?). That's the number that shows the promise came true.

Audience → channel → message → metric, all in one straight line. That line is what a GTM plan is.

### Common mistakes

- **"Let's be everywhere at once."** Spread thin, learn nothing, burn out.
- **Copying a giant's playbook.** A company with a huge budget can do things a small team can't. Borrow principles, not tactics that assume their resources.
- **A message that fits the product but not the channel.** Right idea, wrong room, no reaction.

### Your assignment — "Mini GTM"

Pick a hypothetical product (your own idea, or one we supply). Write **one tight paragraph** covering:

- **Who** it's for (a specific ICP, not "everyone"),
- **Which single channel** you'd start with, and why it fits,
- **The core message angle** for that channel,
- **The one metric** that would prove it's working.

Keep it to a paragraph. The discipline of being specific in a small space is the exercise.

**Rubric**

| Criterion | What we're looking for |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **Coherent chain** | Audience → channel → message → metric all fit together and point the same way. |
| **Realism** | It suits a small, focused team — not a Super Bowl ad. |
| **Sequencing sense** | You start narrow and earn scale, rather than trying to be everywhere. |

Rated **Developing / Solid / Strong.**
